Definition

What is spirituality
in recovery?

Spirituality is not just religion. The WHO defines spiritual wellbeing as a fourth dimension of health encompassing meaning, purpose, identity, connection, transcendence, community, and culture.

Meaning & purpose
Having a reason to stay sober. Knowing what recovery is oriented towards, not just what it is moving away from.
Identity & community
Rebuilding a sense of self beyond substance use. Belonging to something, whether it be a group, a culture, a shared practice.
Connection
To oneself, to other people, to nature, to culture, to something beyond the self. Johann Hari suggests the opposite of addiction is not sobriety; it is connection.
Transcendence
A sense of something larger than oneself, whether spiritual, natural, cultural, or relational. Present across secular and religious frameworks alike.
Why it matters clinically: Cook's systematic review identifies spiritual wellbeing as a significant independent predictor of long-term recovery outcomes and reduced relapse. It is not just a correlation of other protective factors, but a predictor on its own. Yet it is seldom asked about.
Clinical tools

What to ask
and how to ask it.

Two validated brief spiritual history frameworks that integrate into a standard biopsychosocial assessment in a few short minutes. Use them to establish conversation, not to assign a score.

Framework
FICA
Puchalski & Romer, 2000
F — Faith & belief "Do you have spiritual beliefs or a sense of faith that gives your life meaning?" If no: "What gives your life meaning or purpose?"
I — Importance "How important is spirituality in your life and in your recovery?" "Has it influenced how you cope with your situation?"
C — Community "Are you part of a spiritual or faith community?" "Is this a source of support for you?"
A — Address in care "How would you like me to address this in your care?" Use this to have the referral conversation.
Framework
HOPE
Anandarajah & Hight, 2001
H — Sources of hope "What are your sources of hope, strength, comfort, and peace?" "What do you hold onto during difficult times?"
O — Organised religion "Are you part of a religious or spiritual community?" "Does it support you, and how?"
P — Personal spirituality "Do you have personal spiritual beliefs independent of organised religion?" "What aspects of your spirituality or spiritual practices do you find most helpful in your recovery?"
E — Effects on care "Has being unwell affected your ability to do things that usually help you spiritually?" "Are there any aspects of medical care that conflict with your spiritual beliefs?"
When to use FICA vs HOPE: Both are appropriate. FICA is slightly more direct and suits brief consultations. HOPE takes a broader approach and may suit longer assessments or patients where you want to explore sources of meaning before asking about formal belief. Neither requires spiritual expertise — they are conversation openers, not diagnostic tools.

The case that built this resource

A soldier's story.

"I am trying to fill the hole within."

During a placement at a residential rehabilitation centre in rural New South Wales, I worked with an Australian Defence Force veteran who had been deployed to the Philippines following Typhoon Haiyan in 2013. His role was body retrieval. He returned with PTSD but did not seek treatment. Instead, he began using methamphetamine to suppress nightmares. This pattern is consistent with Khantzian's self-medication hypothesis: substances selected specifically for their capacity to manage an intolerable affective state.1

When his use was discovered, he was discharged from the military. That discharge produced a second wound — what Litz and colleagues term moral injury: conscience-based damage to a person's moral and spiritual sense of self.2 He described feeling aimless. Purposeless. Without identity. His substance use escalated across years of repeated detoxifications, each of which addressed the substance without addressing what the substance was for.

At forty-eight, he suffered a heart attack and was resuscitated. He reported a near-death experience — describing it as dying on the table, and what followed as being born again. He began seeking a new identity: one oriented toward meaning rather than away from pain. He turned heavily to spirituality as a framework for reconstructing what substance use had dismantled.

Near-death experiences are documented in the medical literature, occurring in up to 20% of cardiac arrest survivors and consistently associated with reduced fear of death and lasting reorientation of values.3 What happened to this patient was clinically recognisable — post-traumatic growth4 and quantum change.5 And it raises a question: we cannot prescribe a near-death experience. But we can ask about the hole within.

Why this resource exists: Working with this patient — and others — I realised I faced three obstacles. I did not know what to ask. I did not know how to ask it. And I did not know what to do with the answer. This resource is an attempt to address all three.
About this resource

Built during placement.
For students like me.

This resource was developed during a five-week community placement at a residential rehabilitation centre in rural New South Wales, as part of a medical school community placement.

It is designed for medical students working with people with addiction in Queensland and rural New South Wales. It is not a clinical protocol; it is a starting point for a conversation that most people have never been invited to have.
